\begin{abstract}
% =============================================================================
Dying ReLU describes individual units going permanently silent during
training---a unit-level pathology to fix. We study a different, comparative
question: across ordinary, successful training, does the \emph{fraction} of
gradient-carrying units behave the same way in every architecture, or does it
depend on the activation function's shape? Using a hook-based method that
recovers a network's exact pointwise gradient gate $\Gamma(x)=|f'(x)|$
without modifying the architecture, we find it does not generalize. In three
independent ReLU-based architectures (ResNet-18, ResNet-50, VGG-11) across
two datasets, gate density (active-gradient-fraction) declines monotonically
during training in every one of 18 independent runs (sign-test
$p=3.8\times10^{-6}$); in the GELU-based ViT-B/16 it rises in 4 of 4 runs.
Holding a CNN backbone exactly fixed and varying only the activation, GELU,
SiLU, and Mish all reproduce ViT-B/16's rising direction on the same
ResNet-18/VGG-11 skeleton (48 of 48 independent runs, $p=2.44\times10^{-4}$
per activation), ruling out a CNN-versus-Transformer confound, and the
direction survives replacing BatchNorm with GroupNorm (24 runs) and is
unchanged across five gate-density thresholds spanning two orders of
magnitude (zero sign flips in 60 checks). We then derive, rather than only
describe, why: BatchNorm's learned scale shrinks under weight decay and a
cosine learning-rate schedule (an established consequence of training
scale-invariant layers), collapsing the post-normalization pre-activation
variance; whether a shrinking-variance gate converges toward 0 or 1 depends
only on a fixed, activation-specific threshold-crossing point $z_{\rm low}$,
computed once with no training involved. A 13-point smoothness sweep---whose
Softplus($\beta$) family has a derivative \emph{exactly} equal to the
logistic sigmoid used by the project's original synthetic theory---confirms
a monotonic dose-response (Spearman $\rho=-0.71$, $p=6\times10^{-7}$,
seed-level), and a dedicated validation experiment confirms the mechanism's
central, previously-untested assumption directly: the pre-activation mean
drifts to a common small negative band across nine very different
activation functions, and comparing this shared drift to each activation's
independently-computed $z_{\rm low}$ correctly predicts the observed
direction in 9 of 9 cases, including correctly flagging which case (Softplus
at $\beta=50$, sitting within $0.004$ of the critical boundary) should be
the least reliable---which it empirically is. A direct per-channel test
(3{,}904 channels per run, rather than one pooled population statistic)
confirms the same relationship using a $\sigma$-normalized
threshold-distance variable (12 of 12 activation$\times$seed cells,
$p=2.44\times10^{-4}$), and reveals two distinct routes to the same
threshold-crossing logic: $\mu$-drift for ReLU, whose threshold sits at the
live center of its channels' distributions, and $\sigma$-shrinkage for
GELU/SiLU/Mish, whose threshold sits far in the tail. We further test, and
do not assume, generalization: the direction split replicates exactly under
Adam (48 of 48 runs) and on Tiny-ImageNet-200, and the smooth-activation
rise alone replicates on two non-convolutional, LayerNorm-only
architectures (MLP-Mixer, Transformer-Encoder)---but the split collapses
under AdamW's decoupled weight decay (all four activations decline), and
the ReLU-specific decline, unlike the smooth-activation rise, does not
generalize beyond convolutional architectures. We report these as genuine
scope boundaries rather than omitting them. We also report two honest,
non-positive findings rather than omitting them: representational effective
rank rises in every condition tested regardless of activation (114$+$ runs),
giving it no power to discriminate anything in this paper, and using gate
density to guide channel pruning is statistically equivalent to magnitude
pruning for ReLU but significantly \emph{worse} than magnitude pruning for
every smooth activation tested ($p<2\times10^{-4}$). A final, exploratory
probe finds gate stiffness reduces the convergence probability of the iDLG
gradient-inversion attack but provides no protection against the stronger,
scale-invariant Inverting Gradients attack---an attack-dependent effect we do
not claim extends to the real architectures studied above. We report this as
a bounded, mechanistically-explained, activation-class-dependent phenomenon,
not a universal theory of gradient dynamics or trainability.
Code: \url{https://github.com/ahmedElmersawy/Optimization-Landscape-CNN}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================

Whether a neural network can learn depends on which fraction of its
computation graph carries gradient signal backward at all; the local
derivative of each activation function acts as a gate that determines this
for every unit, every layer, every step. It has been known for over a
decade that, for ReLU networks specifically, this gate can close
permanently for individual units during training---the dying-ReLU
phenomenon~\cite{lu2019dying}, familiar since ReLU's original adoption and
accompanied by a long line of remedies (Leaky ReLU, careful initialization,
normalization). \textbf{This paper is not about that observation, and we
state so explicitly at the outset.} We do not claim to discover that ReLU
units lose gradient-carrying capacity during training, and we do not treat
such a decline as a pathology to diagnose or repair. We ask a narrower,
comparative question that a ReLU-specific literature cannot ask by
construction: does the \emph{fraction} of gradient-carrying units change in
the same direction across different activation functions during ordinary
training, or is the direction itself a property of the activation's shape?

We call the fraction of gradient-carrying units, evaluated on a fixed batch
at a given point in training, \emph{gate density}, and it differs from the
traditional dead-neuron framing in three respects. First, it is continuous,
not binary: rather than labeling a unit permanently dead or alive, we
compute the fraction of units whose local gate magnitude
$\Gamma(x)=|f'(x)|$ exceeds a small threshold, re-measured fresh at every
epoch. Second, it is descriptive, not diagnostic: we make no claim that a
network with falling gate density is failing---every network we study is
learning successfully throughout, with test accuracy rising monotonically
alongside the decline we measure. Third, it is defined for \emph{any}
differentiable activation, not only piecewise-linear ones: the dead-neuron
vocabulary presumes a literal zero region that ReLU has and GELU does not,
so a framing built around ``death'' cannot even be applied to a smooth
activation, let alone compared against ReLU on equal terms.

Measuring $\Gamma(x)$ in general requires knowing an activation's
closed-form derivative in advance or modifying the network to expose it,
which is why empirical study of gating dynamics has historically been
confined to ReLU. We remove this requirement: for any elementwise
activation $y=f(x)$, a backward hook's \texttt{grad\_input} is exactly
\texttt{grad\_output} times $f'(x)$, so their ratio recovers the local gate
exactly, for any $f$, on any unmodified architecture (Section~\ref{sec:method}).

Tracking gate density at every epoch of standard supervised training on
CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, we find that its direction of change is not shared
across activation families. In three independent ReLU-based architectures,
gate density falls consistently as test accuracy rises---a direction
consistent with, though far more broadly replicated than, what the
dying-ReLU and activation-sparsity literatures have separately reported for
ReLU alone. In the GELU-based ViT-B/16, gate density rises instead. We then
ask, and answer, the question this contrast immediately raises: is this
about the activation function, or about CNNs versus Transformers? Holding a
CNN architecture exactly fixed and swapping only the activation
(Section~\ref{sec:dynamics}) shows it is the activation. We further rule out
BatchNorm as a necessary condition (Section~\ref{sec:dynamics}) and show the
direction is robust to the measurement threshold across two orders of
magnitude (Section~\ref{sec:dynamics}).

Unlike most papers reporting this kind of phenomenon, we then derive why
(Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}), not only that. BatchNorm's learned scale
shrinks during training as a consequence of training a scale-invariant
layer under weight decay with a decaying learning rate; this collapses the
pre-activation variance for every activation alike. Whether a
shrinking-variance gate concentrates toward 0 or 1 depends only on a fixed,
activation-specific threshold-crossing point, computable with no training
at all. We test this mechanism's central empirical assumption directly
(rather than assuming it) and find it holds, and we test the mechanism's
prediction across a 13-point continuous smoothness sweep, in which one
activation family's derivative is mathematically identical to this
project's original synthetic gate formalism. We then verify the mechanism
at the level of the individual channel rather than only the population
(Section~\ref{sec:channel_mechanism}), and test generalization deliberately
rather than assuming it (Sections~\ref{sec:optimizer}--\ref{sec:generalization}):
the direction split survives Adam and a non-CIFAR, 200-class dataset, and
the smooth-activation rise alone survives two non-convolutional,
LayerNorm-only architectures, but the split collapses under AdamW and the
ReLU-specific decline does not generalize beyond CNNs---scope boundaries we
report directly rather than discover after submission.

Finally, we report two findings exactly as the data shows them, including
where that complicates a clean narrative: representational rank rises in
every condition we tested regardless of activation, which means it cannot
distinguish anything this paper argues for and is reported as a null
result, not a contribution; and using gate density to guide pruning helps
for ReLU but actively misleads for every smooth activation tested. An
exploratory privacy probe closes the paper, scoped explicitly as not
extending to the architectures studied elsewhere.

\paragraph{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{A general, architecture-agnostic method for measuring the
  exact pointwise gradient gate} of any elementwise activation without
  modifying the network, validated against the known ReLU-at-initialization
  baseline across three architectures and 20 seeds.
\item \textbf{Evidence that gate density's direction of change during
  training is activation-class-dependent, not architecture-dependent, and
  is robust to threshold choice.} Consistent decline across 18 independent
  training runs of three ReLU architectures on two datasets; a consistent
  rise across 4 independent runs of GELU-based ViT-B/16; confirmation via an
  architecture-matched ablation (48 independent runs, GELU/SiLU/Mish all
  reproducing the rise on a CNN backbone); confirmation that BatchNorm is
  not necessary (24 runs, BatchNorm vs.\ GroupNorm); and zero sign flips
  across five thresholds spanning two orders of magnitude (60 checks).
\item \textbf{A derived, tested mechanism}, not only a description: training
  shrinks BatchNorm's scale (an established consequence of weight decay on
  scale-invariant layers under a decaying learning rate), collapsing
  pre-activation variance for every activation; the resulting direction is
  governed by each activation's fixed threshold-crossing location
  $z_{\rm low}$. We verify the mechanism's key assumption---a shared
  pre-activation mean drift across very different activations---directly,
  and the resulting sign prediction is correct in 9 of 9 tested
  conditions, correctly anticipating which condition (closest to the
  critical boundary) should be least reliable.
\item \textbf{Direct per-channel verification of the mechanism}, across
  3{,}904 channels per run rather than one pooled population statistic,
  using a $\sigma$-normalized threshold-distance variable (12 of 12
  activation$\times$seed cells, $p=2.44\times10^{-4}$); this reveals two
  distinct routes to the same threshold-crossing logic depending on each
  activation's margin to its threshold.
\item \textbf{Generalization tested, not assumed, along three axes}:
  optimizer (the split replicates exactly under Adam, 48 of 48 runs, but
  collapses under AdamW's decoupled weight decay, 48 of 48 runs all
  declining instead), scale (ReLU's decline and the smooth-activation rise
  both replicate on Tiny-ImageNet-200), and architecture family (the
  smooth-activation rise replicates on two non-convolutional, LayerNorm-only
  architectures, but the ReLU decline and the separate effective-rank-rise
  observation do not). We report all three as genuine, characterized scope
  boundaries rather than omitting the checks that did not fully generalize.
\item \textbf{Two honestly-reported non-findings}: representational
  effective rank rises in every condition tested (114$+$ runs) regardless of
  activation, architecture, dataset, or threshold, giving it no
  discriminating power; and gate-density-guided pruning is statistically
  equivalent to magnitude pruning for ReLU but significantly worse for
  every smooth activation tested.
\item \textbf{An exploratory finding} that gate density's relationship to
  gradient-inversion attack convergence is attack-dependent---protective
  against iDLG, absent against the stronger Inverting Gradients
  attack---demonstrated in a controlled setting and not claimed to extend to
  the architectures above.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Dying ReLU~\cite{lu2019dying}.} Documents that individual ReLU
units can become permanently inactive during training, treated as a
pathology with established remedies. We measure a continuous,
population-level statistic that need not imply permanent death, find it
co-occurs with \emph{rising} accuracy rather than failure, and generalize
the question to activation functions for which ``death'' is not even
well-defined.

\paragraph{Activation sparsity.} Trained ReLU networks are known to develop
increasing forward-pass activation sparsity~\cite{glorot2010understanding}.
We measure backward-pass gate sparsity, which coincides with forward
sparsity for ReLU but not for smooth activations, and track it as an
epoch-resolved trajectory rather than a post-training snapshot.

\paragraph{Lottery Ticket Hypothesis and neural pruning.} The Lottery
Ticket Hypothesis~\cite{frankle2019lottery} identifies sparse,
separately-trainable subnetworks via iterative weight-level pruning and
rewinding. Our gate-density statistic is a continuous, input-dependent,
non-interventional quantity, not a static weight mask, and we make no
prunability claim a priori---Section~\ref{sec:pruning} tests one directly
and reports a negative result for three of four activations.

\paragraph{Rank collapse and Neural Collapse.} Prior work shows
representation rank collapsing with depth in pure attention
stacks~\cite{dong2021attention} and within-class feature variability
collapsing in the terminal phase of training~\cite{papyan2020prevalence}.
We track a different rank statistic (entropy-based effective rank of
per-layer activations), at different layers, across the bulk
(non-terminal) phase of training, and find it \emph{rising}---not a
contradiction of either prior result, since neither tracks gate density or
this regime, but a finding we explicitly do not connect to either
literature beyond noting the vocabulary overlap.

\paragraph{Representation collapse.} In self-supervised learning, encoders
can collapse to low-dimensional or constant
outputs~\cite{hua2021feature,jing2022understanding}. Our usage of
``collapse'' (declining gate density) is unrelated and, on the rank
dimension specifically, opposite in sign (our measured rank \emph{rises});
we flag this explicitly to avoid the vocabulary collision misleading a
reader.

\paragraph{Gradient inversion attacks.} DLG~\cite{zhu2019deep},
iDLG~\cite{zhao2020idlg}, and Inverting Gradients~\cite{geiping2020inverting}
are attack methods we reuse unmodified in Section~\ref{sec:privacy}. That
literature is about attack or defense design; we ask whether an incidental
property of the victim model predicts attack success, finding the answer
is attack-dependent.

\paragraph{BatchNorm scale dynamics under weight decay.} Scale-invariant
layers followed by BatchNorm reach an equilibrium weight norm under SGD
with weight decay that depends on the learning
rate~\cite{vanlaarhoven2017l2,hoffer2018norm}. Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}
applies this to explain why BatchNorm's $\gamma$ shrinks under our cosine
schedule, rather than re-deriving the result from scratch.

\paragraph{Optimization landscapes and inverse problems.} The synthetic
phase-transition theory motivating this project's gate vocabulary (Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic})
builds on loss-landscape visualization~\cite{li2018visualizing}, inverse
problems in imaging~\cite{ongie2020deep,chan2016plug}, feature
inversion~\cite{mahendran2015understanding}, and the analogy to phase
transitions in compressed sensing~\cite{donoho2006compressed}.

\section{Method: Measuring Gradient Gates}
\label{sec:method}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The gate-recovery trick}

For an elementwise activation $y=f(x)$, the chain rule gives, exactly,
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{grad\_input} = \texttt{grad\_output}\cdot f'(x).
\end{equation}
A backward hook registered on any activation module observes both tensors;
their elementwise ratio recovers $\Gamma(x)=|f'(x)|$ exactly, for any $f$,
without knowing its closed form and without modifying the forward pass.
\textbf{Gate density} at threshold $\theta$ is the fraction of elements
with $\Gamma(x)>\theta$ on a fixed evaluation batch, re-measured every
epoch on the \emph{same} batch so that trajectories are not confounded by
changing inputs.

\subsection{Architectures and validation}

We instrument five architectures: CIFAR-native ResNet-18, ResNet-50, and
VGG-11 (a standard 3$\times$3-stride-1 stem, no initial max-pool, since the
canonical ImageNet stem destroys a 32$\times$32 image's resolution in the
first layer), and canonical (unmodified) ViT-B/16 and ConvNeXt-Tiny via
torchvision. We validate the method against the only available
ground truth---ReLU's known $\approx\!50\%$ active-fraction at
BatchNorm-normalized random initialization---across 20 seeds and three
architectures: ResNet-18 $0.555\pm$, ResNet-50 $0.529\pm$, VGG-11
$0.618\pm$ (mean active-fraction at $\theta=0.01$, full distribution in
Appendix~\ref{app:validation}), matching the theoretical expectation.

\subsection{Architecture-fixed activation ablation}
\label{sec:ablation_method}

To separate activation effects from architecture effects
(Section~\ref{sec:dynamics}), we hold the ResNet-18 and VGG-11 skeletons
exactly fixed---identical depth, width, stem, BatchNorm placement, and
parameter count, verified identical across activations---and vary only the
activation module via a single constructor argument. The same mechanism
extends to a BatchNorm-versus-GroupNorm ablation (replacing every
\texttt{BatchNorm2d} with \texttt{GroupNorm}, 32 groups or fewer for narrow
layers) on the identical skeleton.

\subsection{Statistical methodology}
\label{sec:stats_method}

Training-dynamics trajectories span 25 epochs; epochs within one run are
not independent observations of a stochastic process and are never pooled
across seeds for a hypothesis test. \textbf{Every directional claim in this
paper is computed at the level of the independent seed trajectory}: we
compute one Spearman correlation (or one mean/delta) per (architecture,
activation, dataset, seed) combination, then test the \emph{sign} of that
per-trajectory statistic across independent seeds with an exact binomial
sign test. This is the corrected methodology used throughout; an earlier
internal draft pooled epochs across seeds for nominal $p$-values that
inflated significance by roughly the number of epochs and has been
discarded entirely---no number from that draft appears in this paper.

\section{Gate Collapse During Normal Training}
\label{sec:dynamics}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The core direction split}

Training each architecture with SGD (momentum 0.9, weight decay
$5\times10^{-4}$, Nesterov, cosine learning-rate schedule from $0.1$,
batch size 128, 25 epochs), we track gate density and effective rank on a
fixed held-out batch every epoch. Across ResNet-18, ResNet-50, and VGG-11
on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (6 architecture$\times$dataset cells, 3 seeds
each, 18 independent trajectories), gate density falls and effective rank
rises in every single trajectory (sign test, $p=3.8\times10^{-6}$ for
both). Test accuracy rises throughout (e.g.\ ResNet-18/CIFAR-10: 44\%
$\to$ 92--93\%). In the GELU-based ViT-B/16, gate density instead rises
toward a ceiling in 4 of 4 independent runs (3 seeds on CIFAR-10, 1 seed on
CIFAR-100).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[height=0.82\textheight,width=\linewidth,keepaspectratio]{training_dynamics_gate_collapse.png}
\caption{Gate collapse during normal supervised training. Mean
active-gradient-fraction and effective rank vs.\ epoch (shaded: 95\% CI
across seeds) for ResNet-18, ResNet-50, and VGG-11 on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100.
Gate density declines and effective rank rises monotonically in every one
of 18 independent trajectories (sign test $p=3.8\times10^{-6}$, both
statistics).}
\label{fig:training_dynamics}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Resolving the confound: architecture or activation?}
\label{sec:archvact}

A ReLU-vs-ViT-B/16 contrast confounds activation function with
architecture family (CNN vs.\ Transformer), normalization, and several
other structural differences. Holding ResNet-18 and VGG-11 exactly fixed
and swapping only the activation (ReLU, GELU, SiLU, Mish; 3 seeds; both
datasets; 48 independent runs total) resolves this directly: every one of
12 independent ReLU runs declines, and every one of 12 GELU, 12 SiLU, and
12 Mish runs rises instead (sign test $p=2.44\times10^{-4}$ for each
activation's 12-of-12 consistency). \textbf{The direction flips with the
activation, not the architecture family.} Effective rank, in contrast,
rises in all 48 of 48 runs regardless of activation
($p=3.55\times10^{-15}$)---it does not track the activation-class split and
is reported as a separate, non-discriminating observation
(Section~\ref{sec:rank_null}), not folded into this contribution.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{activation_ablation.png}
\caption{Architecture-fixed, activation-varied ablation. ResNet-18 and
VGG-11 skeletons held exactly fixed; only the activation varies. Gate
density falls for ReLU and rises for GELU/SiLU/Mish on the \emph{same}
backbone (top row); effective rank rises for every activation regardless
of direction (bottom row), motivating its exclusion as a contribution.}
\label{fig:activation_ablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{BatchNorm is not necessary}
\label{sec:bn_ablation}

Replacing every BatchNorm layer with GroupNorm on the identical ResNet-18
skeleton (4 activations, 3 seeds, 24 runs) leaves the direction unchanged:
3 of 3 ReLU runs decline and 9 of 9 smooth-activation runs rise under
\emph{both} normalization schemes (Figure~\ref{fig:bn_ablation}). Magnitude
is noisier under GroupNorm, particularly for Mish, and final accuracy is
$\sim$2--4 points lower---both attributable to GroupNorm being unmatched
in hyperparameters at this scale, not evidence about the underlying
mechanism. We conclude BatchNorm is not a necessary condition for the
phenomenon, narrowing what could be driving it to the activation function
itself---though Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} shows BatchNorm's presence
\emph{does} supply the specific mechanism by which the direction is set in
our main experiments.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{bn_vs_gn_gate_dynamics.png}
\caption{BatchNorm vs.\ GroupNorm. The activation-class direction split
(ReLU declining, smooth activations rising) is identical under both
normalization schemes, on the identical ResNet-18 skeleton.}
\label{fig:bn_ablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Threshold robustness}
\label{sec:threshold}

The active-gradient-fraction statistic depends on a threshold $\theta$.
Sweeping $\theta\in\{0.001,0.005,0.01,0.05,0.10\}$ on the architecture-fixed
ResNet-18 ablation (4 activations, 3 seeds, 60 checks) finds \textbf{zero
sign flips}: ReLU declines and every smooth activation rises at every
threshold tested. The \emph{magnitude} of the effect is asymmetric,
however: at the originally-used threshold ($\theta=0.01$), the
smooth-activation rise is small in absolute terms ($<1$ percentage point)
against ReLU's $\sim$13-point fall; the effect only becomes comparably
large at the strictest threshold tested ($\theta=0.10$: $+4.7$ to $+5.5$
points). We report this asymmetry explicitly rather than implying the two
regimes are symmetric.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{threshold_robustness.png}
\caption{Threshold robustness. Active-gradient-fraction trajectories across
five thresholds spanning two orders of magnitude. ReLU declines and every
smooth activation rises at every threshold; magnitude grows with stricter
thresholds.}
\label{fig:threshold_robustness}
\end{figure}

The raw, threshold-free gate magnitude (\texttt{gate\_mean}) \emph{declines
for every activation tested}, including the ones whose thresholded gate
density rises (ReLU $-25.5\%$, GELU $-15.3\%$, SiLU $-9.0\%$, Mish
$-8.4\%$). This is not a contradiction: per-channel quantile analysis shows
the gate-value distribution compresses from both tails toward its center
for smooth activations (e.g.\ GELU's 5th percentile rises from 0.041 to
0.076 while its 95th percentile falls from 1.10 to 0.88 over training),
simultaneously raising the count above a near-zero threshold while lowering
the mean; ReLU's distribution remains exactly bimodal at $\{0,1\}$
throughout, so its mean and thresholded fraction are mathematically
identical and must move together. Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} explains why
this compression happens for every activation but produces opposite
threshold-crossing outcomes.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{gate_distribution_dynamics.png}
\caption{Gate-magnitude distribution dynamics. Quantile bands (1st--99th
percentile) over training, by activation. Smooth activations compress from
both tails toward a moderate center; ReLU remains exactly bimodal
$\{0,1\}$, with training only shifting the mixture weight between modes.}
\label{fig:gate_distribution}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Optimizer generalization}
\label{sec:optimizer}

Every experiment so far uses SGD with momentum and coupled weight decay,
which the mechanism in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} relies on directly
(BatchNorm's scale shrinking toward an equilibrium under weight decay on a
scale-invariant layer). We repeated the architecture-fixed ablation
(ResNet-18, VGG-11; ReLU/GELU/SiLU/Mish; both datasets; 3 seeds; 25 epochs;
48 independent runs per optimizer) under Adam ($\mathrm{lr}=10^{-3}$,
\emph{coupled} weight decay, matching SGD's update structure) and AdamW
(same learning rate, \emph{decoupled} weight decay).

\textbf{Under Adam, the result is unchanged}: every one of 12 independent
ReLU runs declines and every one of 12 GELU, SiLU, and Mish runs rises
(sign test $p=4.88\times10^{-4}$ per activation), at magnitudes matching
SGD (Figure~\ref{fig:optimizer_generalization}).

\textbf{Under AdamW, the split collapses}: all four activations decline
(12 of 12 runs each, $p=4.88\times10^{-4}$), at broadly comparable small
magnitudes rather than ReLU's characteristic large fall against the smooth
activations' small rise. We checked the obvious candidate explanation
directly rather than speculating: BatchNorm's $\gamma$ under AdamW
\emph{itself} splits unexpectedly---ReLU and GELU still shrink (though more
weakly than under SGD/Adam) while SiLU and Mish's $\gamma$ actually
\emph{grows} (3 of 3 seeds each, in both directions)---yet active-gradient
fraction declines uniformly across all four regardless. The simple
$\gamma$-shrinkage account does not explain the AdamW collapse either.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{optimizer_generalization.png}
\caption{Optimizer generalization. Mean $\Delta$active-gradient-fraction
(epoch 24 minus epoch 0), by activation and optimizer, $\pm$ standard error

\label{fig:optimizer_generalization}
\end{figure}

We report this as a genuine, reproducible scope boundary rather than force
a unified account: \textbf{the activation-class direction split, and the
BatchNorm-$\gamma$-shrinkage step of the mechanism, are both empirically
tied to coupled-weight-decay optimizers.} AdamW's decoupled weight decay
produces a qualitatively different, currently uncharacterized regime; we do
not claim the phenomenon or the mechanism extends to it.

\subsection{Generalization beyond CIFAR-scale CNNs}
\label{sec:generalization}

Two further questions remain open after Sections~\ref{sec:dynamics}'s CIFAR-scale
CNN experiments: does the phenomenon hold at larger scale, and is it specific
to convolutional architectures?

\textbf{Scale.} We trained ResNet-18 (its existing CIFAR-native stem, which
keeps an $8\times8$ feature map before the global pool at $64\times64$
resolution rather than collapsing it prematurely) on Tiny-ImageNet-200
(200 classes, $64\times64$, 100k training images), 4 activations, 3 seeds,
25 epochs. ReLU's decline and effective-rank rise replicate exactly (6 of 6
seed-level checks across both metrics, matching CIFAR in both direction and
magnitude). The smooth activations' smaller-magnitude rise also replicates
in net displacement for 9 of 9 (active-gradient fraction) and 8 of 9
(effective rank) seeds, but the trajectories are no longer monotonic at this
scale---an early overshoot followed by partial relaxation---so the
whole-trajectory linear-trend statistic used elsewhere in this paper loses
power on this already-small effect even though the net direction is
preserved. We report both statistics rather than the more favorable one
alone (Appendix~\ref{app:repro} documents the exact comparison).

\textbf{Architecture family.} We built two CIFAR-native, activation-configurable,
non-convolutional architectures with no BatchNorm anywhere---an
MLP-Mixer~\cite{tolstikhin2021mixer} (8 mixer blocks) and a small
Transformer-Encoder~\cite{vaswani2017attention} (6 self-attention blocks,
4 heads)---both using LayerNorm, and instrumented with the same
gate-recovery hooks (every activation is an explicit submodule, unlike
\texttt{nn.TransformerEncoderLayer}'s functional activation, which the
hook-based recovery cannot see). Same 4 activations, both datasets, 3
seeds, 25 epochs.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{generalization_scale_architecture.png}
\caption{Generalization beyond CIFAR-scale CNNs. Mean
$\Delta$active-gradient-fraction (epoch end minus start), pooled across
datasets/seeds. ReLU's decline and the smooth-activation rise both replicate
on Tiny-ImageNet-200 (left). On two non-convolutional, LayerNorm-only
architectures (center, right), the smooth-activation rise still replicates,
but ReLU's decline does not: ReLU rises in MLP-Mixer and is statistically
indistinguishable from zero in the Transformer-Encoder.}
\label{fig:generalization_scale_architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{The smooth-activation rise is not CNN- or BatchNorm-specific}: GELU,
SiLU, and Mish all rise robustly in both new architectures (6 of 6
independent runs each, sign test $p=0.03125$, magnitude $+0.1$ to $+0.8$
points)---this directly answers the motivating question. \textbf{The ReLU
decline, by contrast, does not generalize.} In MLP-Mixer, ReLU instead
rises in 5 of 6 runs; in the Transformer-Encoder it is small and
dataset-dependent (mildly declining on CIFAR-100, mixed on CIFAR-10), with
nothing resembling the large, robust CNN decline. Effective rank is the
most architecture-dependent statistic of all: the ``rises in every
condition regardless of activation'' finding from
Section~\ref{sec:rank_null} does not hold here---MLP-Mixer's rank
\emph{declines} for every activation including ReLU, and the
Transformer-Encoder's rank splits by activation (ReLU rises, the smooth
activations decline). \textbf{The smooth-activation rise generalizes across
architecture and normalization scheme; the ReLU decline and the
universal-rank-rise claim are both more CNN-specific than the original
results alone would suggest}---a narrowing of scope discovered by
deliberately testing outside the CNN family, not assumed to hold.

\section{Mechanism: Why Activation Class Determines the Direction}
\label{sec:mechanism}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The post-BatchNorm pre-activation distribution}

For a channel with pre-BatchNorm output $u$, BatchNorm computes
\begin{equation}
  z=\gamma\cdot\frac{u-\mathrm{mean}(u)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{var}(u)+\epsilon}}+\beta
  \;\;\Rightarrow\;\;
  \mathrm{mean}(z)\approx\beta,\quad \mathrm{var}(z)\approx\gamma^2,
\end{equation}
to first order, by BatchNorm's own definition---not an approximation about
the network, but an exact statement about what BatchNorm computes.

\subsection{Why \texorpdfstring{$\gamma$}{gamma} shrinks}

A convolutional layer immediately followed by BatchNorm is scale-invariant:
rescaling its weights leaves the BatchNorm output unchanged, so only
weight \emph{direction} matters to the forward pass while $\gamma$ alone
sets the effective scale. Under SGD with weight decay on such layers,
prior work~\cite{vanlaarhoven2017l2,hoffer2018norm} shows the effective
scale is pulled toward an equilibrium that scales with $\sqrt{\eta/\lambda}$
for learning rate $\eta$ and weight-decay coefficient $\lambda$; under our
cosine-decayed $\eta$, this equilibrium itself decreases monotonically
across training. This is consistent with, and a plausible explanation for,
$\gamma$'s empirically observed monotonic shrinkage in every one of 12
independent trajectories tested (sign test $p=2.44\times10^{-4}$),
similarly in relative magnitude across activations ($-81\%$ ReLU, $-73$ to
$-74\%$ for GELU/SiLU/Mish).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{bn_gamma_dynamics.png}
\caption{BatchNorm $\gamma,\beta$ dynamics. Mean $|\gamma|$ shrinks
similarly in relative magnitude across all four activations tested (left,
center); per-layer breakdown at epoch 24 (right). Because this trend is
shared across activations, it cannot by itself explain the activation-class
direction split---only the mechanism below does.}
\label{fig:bn_gamma}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The threshold-crossing mechanism}

Define $z_{\rm low}(\theta)=\inf\{z: |f'(z)|>\theta\}$, the point where a
gate crosses $\theta$ on its negative tail---a property of the activation
function and threshold alone, fixed and computable with no training
involved. We compute it exactly via autograd (Table~\ref{tab:zlow}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

the empirically observed gate-density trend.}
\label{tab:zlow}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Activation & $z_{\rm low}$ & Observed $\rho$(epoch, gate density) & Direction\\
\midrule
ReLU / LeakyReLU (all slopes) & $0.000$ & $-0.96$ to $-0.99$ & decline\\
Softplus $\beta=50$ & $-0.044$ & $-0.59$ & decline\\
Softplus $\beta=20$ & $-0.110$ & $+0.73$ & rise\\
Softplus $\beta=10$ & $-0.219$ & $+1.00$ & rise\\
Softplus $\beta=5$ & $-0.439$ & $+1.00$ & rise\\
GELU & $-0.554$ & $+1.00$ & rise\\
SiLU & $-0.912$ & $+0.99$ & rise\\
Mish & $-0.891$ & $+0.97$ & rise\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

If $z\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma\to0$ (Sections
4.1--4.2) and $\mu$ drifts by a \emph{similar} amount regardless of
activation, then $\mathrm{active\_frac}(\theta)=P(|f'(z)|>\theta)\to0$ or
$1$ depending only on which side of $z_{\rm low}(\theta)$ the shared
$\mu$ lands on. Activations with $z_{\rm low}$ near zero have almost no
margin; activations with strongly negative $z_{\rm low}$ have substantial
margin for the same small drift in $\mu$.

\subsection{The shared-drift assumption, tested}
\label{sec:mu_test}

This mechanism is only explanatory, rather than circular, if $\mu$ truly
drifts similarly across activations rather than differently per
activation in a way that tracks the outcome directly. We tested this
rather than assuming it: logging signed pre-activation mean (forward-hook
only, no backward pass) for 9 representative activations spanning the
transition, 3 seeds, 25 epochs. At initialization $\mu$ is small and
positive for every activation ($0.014$--$0.033$); by epoch 6 it has
flipped negative for \emph{every single activation tested} and remains in
a narrow, shared band through epoch 24: $-0.041$ (SiLU) to $-0.066$
(Mish)---a common drift, not nine separate trajectories.

The mechanism is correct on direction for all 9 conditions tested and

direction and open on magnitude and root cause, not as a complete theory.

\subsection{Direct per-channel verification}
\label{sec:channel_mechanism}

Section~\ref{sec:mu_test} tests the mechanism at the \emph{population}
level: one pooled $\mu$ per activation/seed/epoch. This leaves open whether
the same relationship holds at the unit the mechanism actually makes a claim
about---the individual channel. We logged, for every channel of every
gate-instrumented layer in ResNet-18 (4 activations, 3 seeds, 25 epochs,
3{,}904 channels per run), per-channel pre-activation mean and standard
deviation alongside per-channel active-gradient fraction, at the same five
epochs used throughout this section.

A first attempt---correlating each channel's raw margin,
$\mu_c(t)-z_{\rm low}$, against its active-gradient fraction across the 5
logged epochs---gave a confusing, activation-split result: strong and
positive for ReLU (87\% of channels positively correlated, mean
$\rho=+0.61$) but weak and slightly \emph{negative} for GELU/SiLU/Mish
(41--46\% positive, mean $\rho=-0.10$ to $-0.18$). Rather than report this
as a partial failure, we diagnosed it: at epoch 0, ReLU's $z_{\rm low}$ sits
within $0.03$ standard deviations of its channels' own mean---the threshold
lives at the live center of the distribution---while GELU/SiLU/Mish's
$z_{\rm low}$ sits $0.9$ to $1.5$ standard deviations away. $\sigma$ shrinks
about threefold for every activation over training (consistent with
Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}'s variance-collapse argument), while $\mu$ also
drifts slightly negative for every activation (Section~\ref{sec:mu_test}).
For ReLU, this $\mu$-drift directly crosses the live threshold and drives
active-gradient fraction by itself. For GELU/SiLU/Mish, the same small
$\mu$-drift instead pulls the \emph{raw} margin down even as $\sigma$'s much
larger relative shrinkage pulls active-gradient fraction \emph{up} by
thinning the sub-threshold left tail---the quantile-compression effect
already established at the population level in Section~\ref{sec:threshold},
now localized to individual channels. The raw margin conflates these two
opposing forces; it does not capture $\sigma$ at all.

Replacing the raw margin with a $\sigma$-normalized z-score,
$(\mu_c(t)-z_{\rm low})/\sigma_c(t)$---the standard way to ask how many
channel-widths a distribution sits from a boundary, rather than how many raw
units---removes the confound entirely.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{channel_mechanism_zscore.png}
\caption{Direct per-channel mechanism verification. Histograms of the
per-channel correlation between the $\sigma$-normalized margin,
$(\mu_c-z_{\rm low})/\sigma_c$, and active-gradient fraction, across the 5
logged epochs, pooled over 3 seeds (3{,}904 channels per activation per
seed). All four activations show strong, uniform, positive correlation.}
\label{fig:channel_mechanism}
\end{figure}

\textbf{All four activations now show strong, uniform, positive per-channel
correlation}: 90--96\% of channels positively correlated per seed (mean
92--95\% per activation), mean $\rho=+0.66$ to $+0.80$---stronger than the
raw-margin test even gave ReLU. Pooling all 4 activations $\times$ 3 seeds,
12 of 12 show majority-positive channel correlation (sign test
$p=2.44\times10^{-4}$). This is the direct, per-channel confirmation
missing from Section~\ref{sec:mu_test}'s population-level test, and it
sharpens the mechanism rather than merely repeating it: \textbf{thin-margin
activations (ReLU) are driven by $\mu$ crossing a threshold that sits at
their distribution's center; thick-margin activations (GELU, SiLU, Mish)
are driven by $\sigma$-shrinkage thinning a distant tail}---two different
routes to the same threshold-crossing logic, unified by the same
$\sigma$-normalized quantity.

\subsection{A continuous test: the smoothness sweep}
\label{sec:smoothness}

Softplus's derivative is \emph{exactly} the logistic sigmoid,
$\mathrm{softplus}'(x;\beta)=\mathrm{sigmoid}(\beta x)$---the same gate
formalism used by this project's original synthetic theory
(Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic}), with $\beta$ playing the role of that
theory's stiffness parameter $\alpha$. This is not an analogy; it is the
same function. A 13-condition sweep (ReLU; LeakyReLU at four slopes;
PReLU; Softplus at $\beta\in\{50,20,10,5\}$; GELU; SiLU; Mish; 3 seeds; 25
epochs) tests whether smoothness predicts the trend in a continuous
dose-response, using $\mathrm{Var}[f'(x)]$ at initialization on the fixed
evaluation batch as a smoothness index.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{smoothness_sweep_gate_dynamics.png}
\caption{Smoothness sweep. Gate-density trajectories for 13 activation
conditions, colored by hypothesized smoothness group. The sign transition
occurs within the Softplus($\beta$) family, between $\beta=50$ (decline)
and $\beta=20$ (rise)---predicted exactly by Table~\ref{tab:zlow}.}
\label{fig:smoothness_sweep}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{smoothness_vs_gate_trend.png}
\caption{Smoothness index vs.\ gate-density trend. Spearman
$\rho=-0.731$, $p=0.0045$ (condition-level, $n=12$); $\rho=-0.710$,
$p=6.1\times10^{-7}$ (seed-level, $n=37$). One excluded point: PReLU
seed~0 failed to train (10\% accuracy throughout, NaN gate statistics from
epoch 0) and is disclosed, not silently dropped.}
\label{fig:smoothness_correlation}
\end{figure}

The relationship is strong and significant at the seed-trajectory level for
both trend shape ($\rho=-0.710$, $p=6.1\times10^{-7}$) and magnitude
($\rho=-0.605$, $p=5.7\times10^{-5}$), and significant at the
condition level for trend shape ($\rho=-0.731$, $p=0.0045$); the
condition-level magnitude correlation does not individually reach $p<0.05$
at $n=12$ ($p=0.122$) but its bootstrap 95\% CI, $[-0.775,-0.401]$, excludes
zero. One activation---PReLU---is a genuine exception: its smoothness
index at initialization (0.261) is the \emph{highest} of all 13 conditions,
yet it rises. This is not a counterexample so much as a limitation of
measuring smoothness only at initialization: PReLU's negative-slope
parameter is learned and its effective smoothness changes substantially
during training (gate variance falls from $\sim$0.24--0.28 to
$\sim$0.08--0.12), unlike fixed-shape activations. We report this
exception rather than excluding it from the figure.

We use the prescribed hedge throughout: this is \textbf{evidence consistent
with} activation smoothness governing the direction split, supported by a
mechanism whose central assumption we tested directly and whose
predictions matched 9 of 9 cases---not proof, and not a claim that
smoothness is the sole or complete explanation.

\section{Two Honestly-Reported Non-Findings}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Representational rank: a null result}
\label{sec:rank_null}

Effective rank rises in every condition tested in this paper---all 18
original trajectories, all 48 architecture-ablation runs, all 24
BatchNorm/GroupNorm runs, regardless of activation, architecture, dataset,
or threshold (114$+$ independent runs, sign test $p<10^{-14}$ pooled). A
statistic that moves in the same direction in literally every condition
tried has no power to discriminate anything this paper argues for, and we
do not list it as a contribution. We flag, but do not resolve, a possible
connection to BatchNorm-statistics dynamics (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}),
and explicitly distinguish it from Neural Collapse (different layer,
different training-phase window) and self-supervised representation
collapse (opposite sign, unrelated mechanism)---see Related Work.

\subsection{Pruning: gate density is not a general-purpose importance signal}
\label{sec:pruning}

We tested whether end-of-training gate density predicts channel importance
for pruning, using the same ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 checkpoints (4 activations,
3 seeds, global channel pruning across 1920 channels in 8 layers, ratios
10--70\%, no retraining, compared against magnitude pruning and a random
baseline).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{pruning_accuracy_drop.png}
\caption{Gate-density-guided pruning vs.\ magnitude vs.\ random (mean
$\pm$ std across 3 seeds, no retraining). For ReLU, gate-density and
magnitude pruning are statistically indistinguishable. For every smooth
activation tested, gate-density pruning is dramatically and significantly
worse---even at 10\% pruning, GELU/SiLU/Mish all lose 65--80 accuracy
points under gate-density pruning versus under 1.5 points under magnitude
pruning.}
\label{fig:pruning}
\end{figure}

For ReLU, gate-density and magnitude pruning are statistically
indistinguishable (paired-$t$ $p=0.55$)---\emph{informative equivalence}.
For GELU, SiLU, and Mish, gate-density pruning is significantly
\emph{worse} than magnitude pruning at every ratio
($p=1.5\times10^{-4}$, $5.2\times10^{-5}$, $2.3\times10^{-5}$
respectively): at 10\% pruning, removing the lowest-gate-density channels
costs 65--80 accuracy points, while removing the lowest-\emph{magnitude}
channels costs under 1.5 points. \textbf{Gate density should not be
presented or used as a general-purpose proxy for channel importance.} For
three of the four activations studied, doing so is actively harmful, not
merely uninformative.

\section{An Exploratory Privacy Probe}
\label{sec:privacy}
% =============================================================================

As one possible consequence of gate dynamics, we ask whether gate density
affects gradient-inversion attack success, using a controllable victim
model (a small CNN with one tunable-stiffness sigmoid gate, distinct from
the real architectures studied above) against DLG~\cite{zhu2019deep},
iDLG~\cite{zhao2020idlg}, and the stronger, scale-invariant Inverting
Gradients attack~\cite{geiping2020inverting} (7 stiffness values, 20
seeds).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{privacy_gate_collapse.png}
\caption{Gradient-inversion attack convergence vs.\ gate stiffness. Higher
stiffness significantly reduces iDLG's convergence probability (logistic
slope $-0.608$, $p=3.2\times10^{-5}$) but has no effect on Inverting
Gradients (always converges, 100\%).}
\label{fig:privacy}
\end{figure}

Increasing gate stiffness significantly reduces iDLG's convergence
probability (logistic regression slope $-0.608$, $p=3.2\times10^{-5}$) but
has \emph{no} effect on Inverting Gradients, which converges regardless
(100\%; DLG itself shows no significant trend, slope $-0.142$, $p=0.35$).
\textbf{We do not claim this connects to the real-architecture gate
dynamics studied in Sections~\ref{sec:dynamics}--\ref{sec:mechanism}}---this
experiment uses a different, minimal controlled model. The result we do
claim: gate stiffness is not a general-purpose privacy mitigation, and any
claim that gradient sparsity protects privacy should specify which
adversary it was tested against.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
% =============================================================================

\textbf{Scale and architecture family, partially resolved.} The main
results are at CIFAR scale with ResNet/VGG-sized CNNs and one Transformer
(ViT-B/16). Section~\ref{sec:generalization} tests both open questions
directly rather than leaving them as caveats: on Tiny-ImageNet-200
(larger vocabulary, higher resolution), ReLU's decline replicates exactly
and the smooth-activation rise replicates in net direction, though its
already-small magnitude becomes harder to detect with a whole-trajectory
trend statistic once trajectories stop being monotonic at this scale. On
two non-convolutional, LayerNorm-only architectures (MLP-Mixer,
Transformer-Encoder), the smooth-activation rise replicates cleanly, but
\textbf{the ReLU decline does not}---it is small and architecture-dependent
outside the CNN family, as is the separate ``effective rank always rises''
claim. We narrow our claims accordingly: the smooth-activation rise
mechanism generalizes broadly; the ReLU decline and the rank-rise
observation are CNN-specific findings, not general properties of gate
dynamics. Full-scale ImageNet and architecture families beyond these two
remain untested.

\textbf{Optimizer, partially resolved.} The architecture-fixed ablation
replicates exactly under Adam (coupled weight decay, matching SGD's update
structure) but the direction split \textbf{collapses under AdamW}
(decoupled weight decay): all four activations decline instead of
diverging by class (Section~\ref{sec:optimizer}). BatchNorm's $\gamma$
itself behaves unexpectedly under AdamW (splitting by activation in a
pattern that does not track the active-gradient-fraction collapse), so we
do not have a mechanistic account of the AdamW regime---only the empirical
fact that it differs. \textbf{The direction split and the mechanism in
Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} are scoped to coupled-weight-decay optimizers};
we do not claim either extends to AdamW or other decoupled-decay schemes.

\textbf{ConvNeXt-Tiny is excluded from all claims.} A single-seed,
15-epoch run on CIFAR-100 reached only 18.9\% test accuracy (badly
undertrained) and gave a rank-trend sign inconsistent with its own
CIFAR-10 run; we treat this as uninformative, not as evidence for or
against any claim in this paper (full numbers in Appendix~\ref{app:convnext}).

\textbf{The mechanism explains direction, not magnitude or root cause.}
Section~\ref{sec:channel_mechanism}'s per-channel verification strengthens
the directional claim to the level of individual channels, but we still do
not derive why the pre-activation mean drifts negative, nor a closed form
for trend magnitude.

\textbf{Reproducibility.} All training is to a single fixed instrumentation
batch per run for gate measurement; seeds, hyperparameters, and SLURM
configuration are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}. Code and all raw
CSVs/figures are released at the repository linked in the abstract.

\section{Conclusion}
% =============================================================================

Gate density---the fraction of units carrying gradient signal above a
threshold---declines during ordinary training of ReLU-based networks and
rises during ordinary training of GELU/SiLU/Mish-based networks, on
identical architectures, robust to normalization scheme and to the
measurement threshold across two orders of magnitude. This direction split
is not asserted but derived: BatchNorm's scale shrinks under weight decay
and a decaying learning rate, collapsing pre-activation variance for every
activation alike, and whether this collapse drives gate density toward 0
or 1 depends only on a fixed, activation-specific threshold-crossing point.
We tested this mechanism's central assumption directly rather than
assuming it, and its predictions matched the observed direction in 9 of 9
cases across a continuous smoothness sweep whose Softplus family is
mathematically identical to this project's original synthetic gate theory.
A direct per-channel test, across thousands of individual channels rather
than one pooled population statistic, confirms the same relationship at
the unit the mechanism actually describes, once the test variable is
correctly $\sigma$-normalized---and reveals that the mechanism operates
through two different routes depending on activation: $\mu$-crossing for
thin-margin activations (ReLU), $\sigma$-driven quantile compression for
thick-margin activations (GELU, SiLU, Mish). The smooth-activation rise
generalizes beyond CIFAR-scale CNNs, beyond BatchNorm, and beyond SGD/Adam;
the ReLU decline and the effective-rank-rise observation do not generalize
beyond the CNN family, and the entire direction split collapses under
AdamW's decoupled weight decay. We reported, rather than omitted, every
place this story does not extend cleanly: representational rank, which
moves the same way regardless of activation on CNNs and is excluded as a
contribution; pruning, where gate density is actively misleading for three
of four activations tested; and the architecture-, normalization-, and
optimizer-specific boundaries discovered by deliberately testing outside
the original setup rather than assuming it would generalize. The result is
a bounded, mechanistically-explained, activation-class-dependent
phenomenon---not a universal theory of gradient dynamics, and we do not
claim it as one.

\subsection{The synthetic phase-transition theory: motivating context}
\label{app:synthetic}

The gate vocabulary used throughout this paper ($\Gamma(x)=|f'(x)|$,
active-gradient-fraction, gate collapse) originates in an earlier,
fully synthetic study of a single fixed-kernel inverse-reconstruction
problem with a tunable sigmoid stiffness $\alpha$. That study is
self-contained, uses bootstrap confidence intervals, Cram\'er--Rao bounds,
and a depth-compounding theorem whose own follow-up experiment found its
key independence assumption holds only asymptotically ($\alpha\geq40$),
which the original study reports candidly rather than overclaiming. We
preserve it here in full as the originating context for this paper's
vocabulary and, specifically, for the exact mathematical correspondence
between its sigmoid-stiffness parameter $\alpha$ and the Softplus-$\beta$
family used in Section~\ref{sec:smoothness}. \textbf{Its claims are scoped
to the synthetic, fixed-kernel reconstruction setting described below and
are not asserted to transfer to the real, trained-network setting of the
main paper}---the connection is established empirically in

\paragraph{The ViT-B/16 vanishing-gradient finding is an initialization
artifact, not depth-related.} A dedicated falsification sweep across depth
(1--12 layers), initialization scheme (default vs.\ truncated-normal
$\mathrm{std}=0.02$), and batch size (4--64), replicated independently
twice, finds gradients vanish in 100\% of seeds at \emph{every} depth under
default initialization and in 0\% of seeds at every depth under the
standard fix. This is unrelated to, and should not be conflated with, the
training-time direction split studied in the main paper.

\subsection{ConvNeXt-Tiny: excluded from all claims}
\label{app:convnext}

A single-seed, 15-epoch training-dynamics run on CIFAR-10 (47.4\% final
test accuracy) showed effective rank rising (consistent with other smooth
activations); the corresponding CIFAR-100 run, badly undertrained at
18.9\% final accuracy, showed effective rank \emph{falling}. We attribute
the sign disagreement to undertraining, not to a real architectural
effect, and exclude ConvNeXt-Tiny from every quantitative claim in this
paper.

\subsection{Reproducibility}
\label{app:repro}

All training-dynamics experiments use SGD (momentum 0.9, weight decay
$5\times10^{-4}$, Nesterov), cosine learning-rate schedule from an initial
rate of 0.1 (CIFAR-native architectures, batch size 128) or scaled
proportionally for 224-resolution architectures, 25 epochs unless
otherwise noted, 3 independent seeds per condition unless a training
failure is disclosed. Gate-density and rank statistics are computed on a
single fixed 64-example held-out batch per run, re-measured every epoch.
All experiments ran on a single NVIDIA A100-80GB GPU via SLURM; full job
scripts, raw CSVs, and all figures are included in the project repository.

The optimizer-generalization experiment (Section~\ref{sec:optimizer}) uses
identical architecture/data/epoch settings with Adam and AdamW substituted
for SGD, learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $5\times10^{-4}$ (coupled for
Adam, decoupled for AdamW), all other hyperparameters unchanged. The
Tiny-ImageNet experiment (Section~\ref{sec:generalization}) uses the public
Tiny-ImageNet-200 release (200 classes, $64\times64$, 100k train/10k val
images), the same CIFAR-native ResNet-18 stem and SGD configuration as
above, ImageNet-standard per-channel normalization statistics, and the
same random-crop/flip augmentation pattern used throughout this paper
(crop padding scaled to the $64\times64$ input). The sequence-model
experiment uses an 8-block MLP-Mixer (patch size 4, hidden dimension 128,
token-mixing dimension 64, channel-mixing dimension 512) and a 6-block,
4-head Transformer-Encoder (patch size 4, hidden dimension 128, MLP
dimension 256, learnable positional embeddings, a prepended class token),
both LayerNorm-only, $\sim$1M parameters each, otherwise the same SGD
configuration and CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 datasets as the architecture-fixed
CNN ablation. The per-channel mechanism experiment
(Section~\ref{sec:channel_mechanism}) uses the same ResNet-18/CIFAR-10/SGD
configuration as Section~\ref{sec:bn_ablation}, logging per-channel
statistics at the same five epochs $\{0,6,12,18,24\}$ used by
Section~\ref{sec:mu_test}.
